Draft

In the 1980s, NBA teams won about 65% of their home games. Today it’s about 55%, and hosting no longer rescues a weaker team the way it once did. So this is a whodunit: one victim, home court, and a lineup of suspects. Most of them turn out to have an alibi, a couple are guilty, and the biggest culprit of all is the one the data can measure but never quite explain.

The long report is split here into eight shorter reads. Start at the top, or jump to the suspect you came for.

  1. Home Court Is Fading: the case laid out, how much home court has fallen, and why hosting stopped saving the underdog.
  2. The Whistle: the friendly whistle was real in every decade; watch it fade as officiating got fairer and more uniform.
  3. The Three-Point Suspect: the move to the arc is the obvious culprit, so how much of the crime does it actually cover?
  4. The Mystery on the Glass: the single biggest driver is rebounding, and it is the one the data can measure but not explain.
  5. The Alibis: the prime suspects (travel, crowd size, pace, rest, nearly every rule change), nearly all of them with airtight alibis.
  6. The Playoffs Are Different (and the Same): home court still swings a single playoff game, but a best-of-seven absorbs most of it.
  7. The Buildings That Still Bite: why Denver and Utah stay hardest to win in, and why blowouts keep growing as home wins shrink.
  8. How We Know This Isn’t Made Up: the battery of checks built to break the findings, and why they held.

The full picture. Every article is a recut of one long report, and these are the source documents behind all of it.

  • Full Report: the complete findings, every chart and section in one place.
  • One-Page Summary: the four questions answered on a single page.
  • The Investigation: the tests behind the findings, with p-values and confidence intervals.
  • Regression Results: the full statistical output, tables and coefficients.
  • Stats Explainer: a plain guide to the methods used.
  • Stats Tutorial: worked examples that reproduce the key results by hand.

About this series. Most of the writing here is Claude’s; I go back and forth with it until the prose is clear enough that I understand it. The analysis underneath is Python running standard methods, not the language model: Claude interprets the results, but does none of the math. I’ve spent dozens of hours pushing it to be clearer, and I still find that faster than writing it all myself. It won’t read like a human wrote every line, but I hope the intent comes through.

## THE SHORT VERSION

The crowd used to move the whistle. Refs would call a foul on the road player where they might let it go at home — and that 1.2-foul-per-game edge, night after night, added up to a 10-point win-rate advantage across a full season. Now refs call it almost dead even. The crowd is still loud. It just doesn't move the whistle the way it once did.

The 3-point revolution made it worse. The crowd's influence mattered most at the rim, where the foul-or-no-call is most subjective. As offenses moved outside the arc, those moments became rarer — and every possession became higher-variance, which favors the underdog (usually the road team).

Two popular explanations the data rules out: faster pace (the 1984 NBA played at today's speed — home court was 10 points higher) and a more equal league (the Jordan-era 1990s were highly unequal, and the decline was already well underway).

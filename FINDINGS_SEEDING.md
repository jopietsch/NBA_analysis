# Playoff HCA: Is the Decline Real, or Just Seeding?

**The question:** The playoffs give home court to the better-seeded team. As the league has become more competitive, top seeds may simply not outclass their opponents the way they once did. If so, the apparent decline in playoff home win % might be a statistical artifact — fewer talent mismatches producing closer games — rather than home court itself weakening. This analysis tests that directly.

---

## What the Data Shows

Controlling for team quality — the actual gap in regular-season records between the home and visiting team in each playoff game — leaves the year-by-year trend completely unchanged. The decline runs at −0.21 pp/yr with or without the quality control. The 40-year drop in playoff home win % is genuine HCA erosion, not a bookkeeping effect of how lopsided playoff matchups happen to be.

Team quality does explain why playoff HCA is *higher* than regular-season HCA. The home team's record advantage over the visitor is a powerful predictor of individual game outcomes — when you're both the higher seed and playing at home, both edges compound. That's why Games 1, 2, and 5 run at 69–75%. But that static relationship doesn't explain the *trend*. The 1980s had larger seed gaps and higher home win rates; today has smaller seed gaps and lower home win rates — and when you strip out the seed gap, the decline is exactly the same size.

---

## The Seed Gap Has Narrowed — But Independently

The quality gap between home and visiting team in playoff games has in fact shrunk over 40 years, drifting from about +0.017 win-percentage points in the 1984–94 era to roughly +0.008–0.011 more recently (trend p < 0.001). Top seeds are less dominant over their opponents than they were in the 1980s. But this compression moves independently of the HCA decline — the two trends coexist without one explaining the other.

---

## The Floor: What Home Court Is Worth When You're the Underdog

Games 3 and 4 in a playoff series are played at the lower seed's arena — meaning the home team is typically the weaker side. Among the 797 such games where the home team also had a worse regular-season record than the visitor, the home team won 52% of the time. Against a better opponent, on your own court, you still win more often than not. That 2-point edge above a coin flip is the purest read on what the venue itself contributes when quality is actively working against you.

---

## Summary

The playoff home court advantage has declined ~9 points over 40 years. None of that decline is explained by changes in seeding structure or the talent gap between seeds. The arena matters less than it used to for the same reason it matters less in the regular season: referees are calling fairer games, shot selection has converged, and the structural edges that once made playing at home decisively different have eroded. The seeding story is a plausible alternative hypothesis that the data cleanly rules out.
